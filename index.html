GLOBAL ELECTRICITY IN 2019

Global annual electricity consumption is approximately 23,398 billion kilowatt hours. This is the story of what powers our world.

Introduction

Electricity is one of the most powerful forces in our lives. The fundamental principles of electricity generation were discovered during the 1820's by Michael Faraday. Since then the use of electricity to power appliances and technology has become ubiquitous. Electricity is used for heating, cooling, refrigiration, appliances, computers, electronics, machinery, manufacturing and public transportation systems among many others. The generation of sufficient amounts of electricity is therefore, of national and global concern.

Over the past century, the main energy sources for generating electricity have been fossil fuels, hydroelectricity, and nuclear energy. Over the last few decades, the strong growth of renewable sources have shifted attention to the concern of pollution which arises as a result of electricity generation. Despite concern over carbon dioxide emissions due to electricity consumption and generation, fossil fuels continue to remain dominant. In 2019, fossil fuels generated 64.5% of the worldwide electricity.

Fossil Fuels

“Present annual world consumption is about equal to the annihilation energy of 4 tons of matter” - Barney Oliver

Fossil fuels include coal, petroleum, natural gas, oil shales, bitumens, tar sands and heavy oils. Fossil fuel power plants, burn fossil fuel materials in order to create heat which is in turns used to generate steam which drives turbines generating electricity. When fossil fuels are burned, they release large amounts of carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas, into the air. Greenhouse gases trap radiating heat into the atmosphere and overtime cause the temperature of the earth to rise. The adjacent choropleth map indicates the global use electricity sourced from fossil fuels. Please hover over countries in order to see more specific data.

Globaly, fossil fuel contributes to over 60% of global electricity generation. This number has significantly dropped from the beginning of the century by around 20%. According to some sources, it is estimated that oil reserves will run out in 47 years, natural gas in 52 years and coal in 133 years.

Renewable energy sources are naturally replenishing and virtually inexhastible in duration. They are however limited in the amount of energy that is available per unit of time and can be severely flow limited. The major types of renewable energy sources are biomass, geothermal, wind and solar. Hydropower is also a renewable source but is not presented here.

The environmental benefit of using renewable energy sources is offset by the difficulties encountered in establishing reliable power generation. Solar panles and wind turbines can have a large land footprint and can impact the environment significantly. Biomass power is abundant but requires a lot of energy to produce and can result in air pollution.

Renewable sources are a focus of intense research and are expected to become more prominent and efficient within the coming decades. The impact of renewable energies is not only predicted to protect the environment but will also be comparatively cheaper.Almost a billion people worldwide live without a reliable source of electricity and renewable sources look set to change this .

“[I predict] the electricity generated by water power is the only thing that is going to keep future generations from freezing.That time is not in the very distant future.” — CHarles Proteus Steinmetz

Hydroelectricity is electrical energy generated when falling water from reservoirs or flowing water from rivers, streams or waterfalls (run of river) is channelled through water turbines.Hydropower is the most widely used renewable energy source. It pairs well with other renweable sources and is a renewable energy source. Hydropower is expensive to run upfornt and cannot be implemented in areas where the geography is not favourable. Hydropower also presents some environmental impacts as the overwhelming majority of hydropower plants require the building of a dam. This construction often changes the surrounding environment significantly.

Hydropower is clean and renewable and certain designs are able to meet peak demand. It relies heavily on local hydrology and geography and is not always a viable implementation. According to some sources, it is estimated that hydropower can reduce cummulative greenhouse gas emissions by 5.6 gigatonnes.
