GLOBAL ELECTRICITY IN 2019

Global annual electricity consumption in 2019 was approximately 23,398 billion kilowatt hours. In the same year, CO2 emissions totaled 33 gigatones.

This is the story of the consequences of what powers our world.

Introduction

Electricity is one of the most powerful forces in our lives. The fundamental principles of electricity generation were discovered during the 1820's by Michael Faraday. Since then the use of electricity to power appliances and technology has become ubiquitous. Electricity is used for heating, cooling, refrigiration, appliances, computers, electronics, machinery, manufacturing and public transportation systems among many others.

Over the past century, the main energy sources for generating electricity have been fossil fuels, hydroelectricity, and nuclear energy. Over the last few decades, the strong growth of renewable sources have shifted attention to the concern of pollution which arises as a result of electricity generation. Despite concern over carbon dioxide emissions due to electricity consumption and generation, fossil fuels continue to remain dominant. In 2019, fossil fuels generated 64.5% of the worldwide electricity.

What are the consequences of our electricity generation? To what degree do our methods harm the environment? And what are the drivers behind the methods we employ to source our energy. The following visualisation explores elextricity in a global context for the year 2019.

Fossil Fuels

“If we keep pulling death from the ground, we will reap death from the skies.” - Van Jones

Fossil fuels include coal, petroleum, natural gas, oil shales, bitumens, tar sands and heavy oils. Fossil fuel power plants, burn fossil fuel materials in order to create heat which is in turns used to generate steam which drives turbines generating electricity.

When fossil fuels are burned, they release large amounts of carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas, into the air. Greenhouse gases trap radiating heat into the atmosphere and overtime cause the temperature of the earth to rise. The adjacent choropleth map indicates the global use electricity sourced from fossil fuels.

Notably, North Africa, the Middle East and much of Eastern Africa rely heavily on the use of fossil fuels for energy generation.

* Data is not available for countries in grey.

Globaly, fossil fuel contributes to over 60% of global electricity generation. This number has significantly dropped from the beginning of the century by around 20%. According to some sources, it is estimated that oil reserves will run out in 47 years, natural gas in 52 years and coal in 133 years.

Global CO2 Emissions

"We know now, the world is not a commodity." - Francois Hollande

According to the World Resources Insisitute, globally, the primary source of greenhouse gas emissions is the generation of electricity. Energy production of all types accounts for 72% of all emissions.

Interestingly, although Northern Africa, Eastern Asia and the Middle East rely most on fossil fuels, with the exception of Saudi Arabia, Omand and the UAE, the largest CO2 emissions are Australia, Canada, Russia, Germany, the US, Japan and Greenland.

Countries such as India and China, the most populated regions in the world do not produce the largest amount of CO2 emissions per person.

* Data is not available for countries in grey.

The choropleth map indicates that although some regions of the world rely most on fossil fuels for their energy production, they are not as likely to also have the highest rates of CO2 emissions. Top Contributors

“The United States could dramatically reduce its carbon emissions per kilowatt-hour without raising its overall energy bill.” — Joseph J. Romm

Hydroelectricity is electrical energy generated when falling water from reservoirs or flowing water from rivers, streams or waterfalls (run of river) is channelled through water turbines.Hydropower is the most widely used renewable energy source. It pairs well with other renweable sources and is a renewable energy source. Hydropower is expensive to run upfornt and cannot be implemented in areas where the geography is not favourable. Hydropower also presents some environmental impacts as the overwhelming majority of hydropower plants require the building of a dam. This construction often changes the surrounding environment significantly.

Hydropower is clean and renewable and certain designs are able to meet peak demand. It relies heavily on local hydrology and geography and is not always a viable implementation. According to some sources, it is estimated that hydropower can reduce cummulative greenhouse gas emissions by 5.6 gigatonnes.
